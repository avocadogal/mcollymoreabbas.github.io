How You can Help

Where to Start?

Recycling used and un-used items is always a good way to protect the environment from the harmful effects of landfills and litter, but knowing what to recycle and where to recycle can be quite the task if you don't know where to start. For starters, it's important to know what to recycle and where to recycle it. Often times it's advised to not collect certain recyclable materials at your home, as they may contain hazardous materials, so you'll have to know where to bring those materials so that they can be recycled properly and safely. Additionally, recycling is a big task and can be expensive (just being real), but just know that it is well worth the effort. Filling up our landfills with waste may be cheaper now, but we'll have to pay for it later. So be green, because so much can be gained from pursuing a less wasteful lifestyle. Recycling is just one way, of many, that you can ensure a better future for us and the planet.

Below are some helpful pieces information regarding appropriate recycling techniques as well as a table that should give you an idea about what can be picked up by the your local recycling service, what needs to be brought to your local center, and what materials aren't recyclable, but can be composted instead. What do the numbers mean?

  • #1 – PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)
    • One of the most common types: bottles (water, juice, mouthwash), jars
    • Can be recycled at home
    • Meant for one-time use; bacteria can grow on them, so don’t reuse
  • #2 – HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)
    • Hard plastic - milk jugs, toys, detergent bottles
    • Also recyclable at home
    • Reuseable
  • #3 - PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)
    • Soft flexible plastic - window frames, watering hoses, clear food wrapping, cooking oil bottles, children’s and pets’ toys
    • “Poison plastic” - contains many toxins that can be leached into the soil and affect the environment
    • Is not recyclable, but can be repurposed (do not reuse with food or for children)
  • #4 - LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene)
    • Found in shrink wrap, garment bags, squeezable bottles, and plastic bags
    • May be reused (less toxic than other plastics)
    • Check with local collection service to see if they recycle LDPE items
  • #5 – PP (Polypropylene)
    • Tough, lightweight, heat resistant plastic - pails, plastic bottle tops, yogurt containers, straws, packing tape
    • Only about 3% of PP products are currently being recycled (increasing)
    • Safe for reuse
    • Check with local collection service to see if they recycle PP items
  • #6 – PS (Polystyrene)
    • Lightweight, shapeable plastic - styrofoam cups, “clamshell” take out containers, egg cartons, packing peanuts
    • Structurally weak - break apart easily and leach chemicals like styrene into the environment/food products
    • Cannot currently be recycled
    • Accounts for 35% of US landfill material
    • Some places that reuse polystyrene
      • Mailboxes Etc.
    • In general, Try to avoid use
  • #7 – Other (BPA, Polycarbonate and LEXAN)
    • Catch all category for polycarbonate and “other” plastics - baby bottles, sippy cups, water bottles, car parts
    • Reuse and recycling protocols are not standardized
    • “PC” labeled plastic items contain BPA
    • Include compostable plastics - marked with “PLA” (do not recycle)
    • Not for reuse unless marked with "PLA"
  • Clear glass
  • Brown glass
    • Beer bottles
  • Green glass
    • Wine bottles
  • Aluminum cans are 100% recyclable. After emptying the cans, you can throw them into a recycling bin or take them to scrap yards or recycling centers, where you can trade them in for cash.
    • Soda cans
  • Aluminum foil is also recyclable. Before placing it into the recycling bin, clean off any pieces of food or leftover residue.
  • Steel cansand tin cans are actually the same thing; tin cans are mainly steel with a thin layer of tin to coat their interior, preserving the flavors of the food inside. Steel cans cannot be traded for cash, but they are recyclable.
    • Soup cans, vegetable cans, tuna cans
  • Car batteries
    • Can be taken to automobile dealers, auto parts shops, or service shop
    • Can be taken to scrap yards for money
  • Household and button batteries
    • Consider using rechargeable batteries if you use batteries often
    • Can be brought to local household hazardous waste locations
    • Cities may also hold household hazardous waste collection days
    • Alkaline batteries may be thrown in the trash in most states (it’s illegal in California) but are highly encouraged to be recycled
    • If you have many batteries to recycle, there are mail-in programs where you can purchase a box to store used batteries and mail it back for the organization to recycle
      • WM Think Green From Home Dry Cell Battery Recycling Kit
      • The Big Green Box
    • Rechargeable batteries
      • Laptop batteries, batteries for cordless appliances
  • Incandescent light bulbs, halogen light bulbs, and LEDs can be thrown in the trash
  • Compact fluorescent lights (CFL) and fluorescent tubes contain mercury, which can be toxic, so they cannot be thrown in the trash
  • Bring to hardware stores or household hazardous waste locations to recycle
    • Some also accept LEDs and incandescent and halogen light bulbs
    • Can also request local waste collection organizations to pick them up
  • Can also purchase mail-in recycling kits

Remember:

  • Flatten recycled items for easy handeling and storage later on
  • Check your area's recycling guidlines
  • If you're not sure about a particular item, don't recycle it
  • Bring your recyclables to places that actually recycle them; check for downcycling
  • Remember to clean/ empty glass bottles before recycling
  • Some curbside recycling programs may require you to sort the glass by color
  • Broken and unwashed glass cannot be recycled at home; bring to a recycling center
  • Other things that can't be recycled at home:
    • Ceramics
    • Heat-resistant glass
    • Mirror/window glass
    • Crystal
